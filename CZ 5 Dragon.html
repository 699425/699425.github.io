Dragon (辰龙)

1988, 2000, 2012, 2024, 2036

Introduction

Dragon is the 5th animal in the 12-year cycle of the Chinese zodiac signs, coming after the Rabbit and before the Snake. Recent years of the Dragon include 2024, 2012, 2000, 1988, 1976, 1964, and 1952, with the next Dragon year in 2036 (Year of the Fire Dragon). It is associated with the earthly branch symbol chen.


Dragon is powerful, endlessly energetic and full of vitality, goal-oriented yet idealistic and romantic, and a visionary leader. They know exactly who they are and possess the keenest sense of self among the 12 zodiacs of Chinese astrology.

How did Dragon become a Chinese zodiac sign?

Legend has it that in ancient times, the dragon was hornless. Proud of its robust physique and remarkable abilities to fly and swim, it was eager to be among the zodiac signs and even aimed to supplant the tiger as the king of beasts. This led to an epic battle between the dragon and the tiger on the earth, with the outcome remaining undecided. Noticing this, the God of Heaven Jade Emperor summoned both the dragon and the tiger to the Heavenly Palace. On the journey there, the dragon, conscious of its lack of the tiger's imposing presence despite its height, received a suggestion from the centipede: borrow the rooster's horns to enhance its grandeur. Initially hesitant, the rooster finally agreed to lend its horns after the dragon's solemn oath and the centipede's guarantee. In the Heavenly Palace, the Jade Emperor, impressed by the imposing demeanors of both the dragon and the tiger, decreed that they would both be crowned kings of beasts - the tiger as the ruler of all land animals, and the dragon as the sovereign of all aquatic creatures. Also, the Jade Emperor consented to include them both in the zodiac.


After returning, the dragon refused to give the horns back to the rooster, fearing that it would lose its majesty without the beautiful horns. Then the dragon dived into the deep sea and never came back to land. Since the rooster couldn't enter the deep water to retrieve them, it turned to the centipede for help. However, the centipede replied, “You still have to ask the dragon for your horns. If he refuses, there's nothing I can do.” Then, the centipede also hid itself. From then on, the rooster was left without horns, only having its red comb, while the dragon owned the beautiful horns.


Given the dragon's status as the sole divine creature, one might wonder why it isn't the leader of the twelve Chinese zodiac signs, instead ranking fifth, after the rat, ox, tiger, and rabbit. In fact, the twelve zodiac signs are personifications of the twelve Earthly Branches. The Earthly Branch “Chen” corresponds to the third lunar month, a time when newly transplanted field seedlings and freshly sown crops are in desperate need of rain for growth. Ancient people regarded the dragon as the deity in charge of thunder and rain. Hoping for favorable weather and abundant harvests, they pinned their hopes on the divine dragon to bring rain. Thus, the dragon is associated with “Chen”. Since “Chen” holds the fifth position among the twelve Earthly Branches, the dragon also takes the fifth place among the twelve zodiac signs.
