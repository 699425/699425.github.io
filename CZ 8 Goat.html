Goat (未羊)

1991, 2003, 2015, 2027, 2039

Introduction

Goat is the 8th animal in the 12-year cycle of the Chinese zodiac signs, coming after the Horse and before the Monkey. Recent years of the Goat include 2015, 2003, 1991, 1979, 1967, 1955, and 1943, with the next Goat year in 2027 (Year of the Fire Goat). It is associated with the earthly branch sign wèi.


Goat is gentle and mild, sensitive and content, and may suffer bouts of pessimism when facing adverse circumstances. They're passive and loyal in love, emotionally dependent on their partner, and prioritize the spiritual over the material.

Is it the Year of Ram, Sheep, or Goat?

In Chinese, the character “羊” (yáng) can refer to both sheep and goats, and is often translated as "goat" in English. However, in Chinese culture, the animal represented by this character is more commonly associated with sheep rather than goats. Therefore, while the English translation may use "goat," it is more accurate to refer to it as "sheep" in the context of the Chinese zodiac.
The distinction of Sheep, Goat, or Ram about the Chinese zodiac year of “羊” (yáng) has caused confusion to the English speaking world, as in the Chinese language, the character Yang can refer to all the ruminants. In fact, according to some professional studies, it is more likely to be the Year of the Goat as the goat is more common in Chinese ancient society. However, Chinese people don't care too much about the choices of words. The images of Ram, Sheep, or Goat can all be found on the Chinese New Year stamps, paintings, and paper-cuts. Thus it has become a personal preference. The lovely and fluffy Sheep is favored by most people while Ram and Goat are choices for those who like strong and tough characteristics.

How did Goat become a Chinese zodiac sign?

There is an interesting legend regarding the sheep's inclusion in the Chinese zodiac. In ancient times, there was no grain on the earth and humanity had to live on vegetables and wild grass, resulting in bony appearances and sallow complexions. One autumn, a divine sheep went down from the Heavenly Palace to the earth and noticed the weak and malnourished state of humanity. Learning that there were no grains on the earth, the divine sheep resolved to bring humanity the seeds of the five grains. Under the cover of night, it stealthily entered the imperial fields in the Heavenly Palace, gathered the seeds of rice, millet, wheat, beans, and sorghum in its mouth, and carried them back to the human world. After giving the seeds to humanity and teaching them how to plant, the divine sheep returned to the Heavenly Palace. Humanity sowed the seeds, and that year, the crops grew. After the harvest, humanity held a grand ceremony to express their gratitude to the divine sheep.


However, when the God of Heaven Jade Emperor learned of this deeds, he felt angry at the divine sheep's offense to him, and ordered its slaughter on the earth, instructing humans to eat its meat. The following year, where the divine sheep was executed, green grass sprouted first, followed by the appearance of lambs. From then on, sheep multiplied on the earth, feeding on grass and providing humans with meat and milk.


When the Jade Emperor decided to select twelve animals as the Chinese zodiac, humans unanimously recommended the sheep. Although the Jade Emperor was still angry about the sheep's theft of grain seeds, he could not oppose the will of so many humans and had to acquiesce to the sheep's inclusion.


As for why the sheep is ranked eighth in the Chinese zodiac, the explanation lies in the ancient Chinese timekeeping method. There are twelve time slots and the eighth period from 13:00 to 15:00 is called “Wei”. It is the optimal time to feed the sheep, so they chose the sheep to represent the “Wei” period. When coming to the order of the zodiac animals, they then gave the sheep the eighth position.
